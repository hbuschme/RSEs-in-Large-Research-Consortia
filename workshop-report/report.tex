\abstract{% Abstract of the article
In the German research funding landscape, so-called Collaborative Research Centres (CRCs, or SFBs in German) play a crucial role in the competitive funding steered by the German Research Foundation.
Digital tools have become so useful in academic research that more and more processes in the research cycle require their use.
This is not different for CRCs, where it has become commonplace to pool tasks related to digital tools in dedicated units, the so-called INF projects.
This report tries to catalogue the INF projects in Germany's research landscape.
Building on that and on a workshop held at the de-RSE26 conference, we collect lessons learnt across INF projects and distil the discussion into guides that other INF projects (or similar structures) can follow.
}
\keywords{INF, CRCs, DFG, RDM, RSE, SFB}

\section{Introduction}

Collaborative Research Centres (CRCs), funded by the German Research Foundation (DFG), are a cornerstone of Germany's third-party funding landscape, bringing together researchers from different disciplines around shared scientific questions over periods of up to twelve years.
As digital tools have become central to the research process – from data collection and analysis to publication and long-term archiving — CRCs have increasingly pooled these responsibilities in dedicated sub-projects known as INF projects (German: "Informationsmanagement und Informationsinfrastruktur"), formally also known as Z-Projects (German: "Zentralprojekt").
These units provide research data management (RDM) and research software engineering (RSE) services to all projects within a CRC, while contending with the particular demands of interdisciplinary, long-running collaborations.
Despite their growing numbers, INF projects are rarely documented as a collective: their structures, practical approaches, and accumulated experience tend to remain within individual CRCs.
This report attempts to change that.
We first catalogue current and past INF projects across Germany's research landscape, before drawing on a workshop held at de-RSE26 to collect lessons learnt and distil them into practical guidance for INF projects and comparable structures.

\section{History of the INF projects}
\label{sec:history}
The beginnings of specialised, shared positions within CRCs can be traced back to the foundation of the CRC 299, which was established in 1997 to survey the use of land under various characteristics.
Being interdisciplinary and already aiming for the maximum runtime of 12 years, it was apparent from the beginning \cite{mueckschel2006} that a careful management of data had to happen in order to be useful for all associated disciplines.
Around the same time, various incidents of scientific misconduct prompted the German Research Foundation (GRF, DFG) to highlight in their
memorandum on ``Safeguarding Good Scientific Practice'' \cite{DFG1998}
the importance of treating research data as ``primary data'', putting out the recommendation to take special care of those data at the host institution.
Combining both requirements, more CRCs started to include personnel to handle data management.

A first workshop \cite{SFB299-2004, SFB299-2005} on comparing lessons learnt was organised by CRC 299 and CRC 552 in 2004 in Gießen.
The respective follow-up workshop was organised again by CRC~299 and CRC~552 in 2007 \cite{SFB299-2007}.
Around the same time, the process of including dedicated projects for the information infrastructure was formalised (based on the precursor work of CRC~299 and CRC~552).

Since 2007, INF projects have been eligible for funding in CRCs at the GRF, with the first projects starting around 2009 \cite{Forschungsdateninfrastruktur2013}.
The focus then shifted to Cologne. In 2009 TRR~32 and CRC~806 organised a workshop of nearly 80 participants of diverse disciplines, 
later documented in a Post-Proceedings volume \cite{curdt787}.
A subsequent workshop by the project ``\begin{de}Radieschen (Rahmenbedingungen einer disziplinübergreifenden Forschungsdateninfrastruktur)\end{de}'' 
in Göttingen \cite{Forschungsdateninfrastruktur2013} found that most INF projects cooperated with central institutions such as libraries and computing centres and maintained central RDM infrastructures.
And, again, acceptance of the INF projects was brought up.\\

Between 2013 and 2015, the number of funded INF projects increased, and hence the funding line became more established \cite{Engelhardt2013Forschungsdatenmanagement, bastian_2025}.
Another follow-up workshop was held in Göttingen in 2018 \cite{Roertgen2019},
organised as part of the project GRAcE  - \href{https://data.goettingen-research-online.de/dataverse/GRAcE}{Göttingen Research Data Exploratory - management parameters derived from the
Göttingen eResearch Alliance}.

Around 2022, the DFG started to hand out guidelines for the INF projects \cite{bastian_2025}.
Another workshop that should belong in this list was organised by the DINI/nestor AG Forschungsdaten in 2020 \cite{Rizzolli_2021},
which discussed how much the tasks of an INF project align with the tasks of data stewards.
Another workshop in Cologne, hosted \cite{bastian_2025} and organised by the DINI/nestor AG Forschungsdaten and the state initiatives
 \href{https://www.uni-marburg.de/en/hefdi/about-hefdi}{HefDi} and \href{https://saxfdm.de/}{SaxFDM},
 noted first, that no participant had been to any of the previous workshops, and second, that following the rules is still a better motivator for following data
 management guidelines, rather than any intrinsic motivation from the scientists.

In March 2026, a de-RSE26 workshop showed an equal presence of Research Software Engineers and Research Data Managers.
Already early on~\cite{mueckschel2006} it was noted that central challenges of data management solutions would be
\begin{itemize}
\itemsep-0.5em
 \item personnel fluctuations over the runtime of a CRC,
 \item safe storage for the lifetime of a CRC,
 \item adaptation to the scientists' questions over the evolution of their research
 \item extensibility and openness of any system,
 \item knowledge management and sharing,
 \item acceptance by the users,
 \item leadership support
\end{itemize}
of any measure of the information system administered by the data stewards.
These are topics that persist till today,
and we try to find out which answers the current generation of INF projects is finding.

\section{Descriptions of INF Projects}
\label{sec:inf-in-germany}
In this section, we describe the various INF projects, how they see themselves and which duties they perform.
In this manuscript, we exclusively refer to these projects as INF projects, but within the respective CRCs, also other acronyms are used.

\subsection{Project Z03 --- Sustainable code development and optimization on modern supercomputing architectures, \href{https://gepris.dfg.de/gepris/projekt/258499086?language=en}{CRC~1170 - Topological and Correlated Electronics at Surfaces and Interfaces, ``ToCoTronics''}, Universität Würzburg}
Established in 2015 as a support unit for the theoretically working projects of the CRC the focus was on numerical methods for Quantum Monte Carlo methods, functional renormalisation group techniques and non-equilibrium Green's function techniques. These methods are usually used on
Germany's High Performance Computing (HPC) systems. Therefore the tight integration between software development, deep knowledge about the methods used in the CRCs science and knowledge about the targeted platforms became paramount to this INF project staffed by one person.
To facilitate the spread of knowledge, regular teaching opportunities were organised based on the materials of \href{https://carpentries.org/}{the Carpentries} in collaboration with the local biology department.
From the very beginning, also the maintenance of the local IT infrastructure of the physics department was part of the project which included
storage systems, desktops, as well as an HPC system.
This integration of maintaining the IT infrastructure while at the same time focusing on sustainable development
facilitated the roll-out of a local gitlab instance for the entire department which has grown by 2026 to $\approx 1000$ users from all
over the world. Networking opportunities were used by participating in the RSE movement, which included the formation of the \href{https://de-rse.org/de/index.html}{de-RSE} society in 2018.
This broad network enabled the project to write the data management strategy of the \href{https://www.ctdqmat.de/}{EXC~2147} which was successfully reviewed in 2019. This strategy was aligned with the FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable data) principles \cite{WilkinsonDumontier2016}.
The continued usefulness of this project was acknowledged by the DFG by granting a second funding round of the CRC~1170 in 2019.
2020 was used for the formation of a local chapter \href{https://de-rse.org/chapter/wue/}{``Wü-RSE''} to provide a place
locally for like-minded people. The project was extended in 2023 for the last funding period. A highlight for this project's visibility included the hosting of de-RSE24, bringing the community of RSEs to Würzburg. Embedded into the community of RSEs, this project contributed
to numerous position papers\cite{Anzt:2020,Goth:2025,gi2025,Kempf2026} over the years.
This project spans the breadth of digital tools in science from a single machine up to HPC clusters, from data generation, over data analysis up to data storage, collaborating with single scientists as well as larger research groups.
This required a flexibility in the tooling, which obviously used git, but led to the use of C++, Python, Mathematica, Fortran, and Julia for programming.
The CRC~1170 and EXC~2147 are supporters of the \href{https://www.fairmat-nfdi.eu/fairmat/}{FAIRmat} ``Nationale Forschungsdateninfrastruktur''(NFDI) consortium, so the \href{https://nomad-lab.eu/nomad-lab/nomad.html}{NOMAD} oasis is being rolled out to
the users in the department.

\subsection{Project INF --- Research Data Management and Research Software Engineering, \href{https://gepris.dfg.de/gepris/projekt/327154368}{CRC~1313 - Interface-Driven Multi-Field Processes in Porous Media – Flow, Transport and Deformation}, University of Stuttgart}
CRC 1313 was established in 2018 to study interface-driven processes in porous media.
To manage the data and code generated by these studies, a task force named "Software and Data" was formed to develop a long-term strategy.
This work led to the creation of the INF project, which began in 2022 during the second funding phase.
The project focuses on RDM and RSE to ensure that data and software outputs follow the FAIR principles.
By reviewing datasets and providing support for software development, the project helps researchers produce high-quality, reusable, and well-tested code.
These efforts are supported by training programs and outreach activities.
The project is well-integrated into both national and local networks, including \href{https://nfdi4ing.de/}{NFDI4Ing}, \href{https://de-rse.org}{de-RSE}, and Data Stewardship goes Germany.
At the local level, INF has built strong connections with the \href{https://www.simtech.uni-stuttgart.de/communication/events/workshops/sigdius-seminars/}{SIGDIUS} network and the central IT service facility of the University of Stuttgart. While some practices were adopted quickly, such as using quality badges and publishing datasets in the \href{https://darus.uni-stuttgart.de/}{DaRUS repository}, others proved more difficult.
For example, implementing Electronic Lab Notebooks (ELNs) (\eg~\elabftw) has been challenging because it requires a significant change in daily research habits.
A major lesson learnt is that technical infrastructure is not just about tools, but also about people. To convince researchers to adopt new practices while facing tight deadlines, the project must demonstrate clear, short-term benefits.
Ultimately, success depends on effective communication and building collaborations that show how better data and software management improves research efficiency.

\subsection{Project INF --- Collaborative and Sustainable Research Data Management, Analysis and Visualisation, \href{https://gepris.dfg.de/gepris/projekt/372486779}{CRC~1340 -- Matrix in Vision}, Berlin} \label{sec:sfb1340}
The INF project of CRC 1340 “Matrix in Vision” originated in medical informatics and radiology at Charité – Universitätsmedizin Berlin and the Berlin Institute of Health (BIH).
It was conceived as a central service project for a data-intensive CRC aiming to understand how pathological changes in the extracellular matrix can be visualised in vivo, particularly in inflammatory disease settings, by combining biophysical and molecular imaging approaches.
Within this context, the INF project provided a shared digital backbone for collaboration, data integration, RDM, secure data exchange, and reusable analysis workflows, especially for multimodal imaging, experimental data, and clinical data.
In practice, the project focused mainly on RDM, complemented by infrastructure and support tasks.
A central outcome was the self-hosted Matrix-COOP platform at Charité, based on Nextcloud, with about 100 TB managed storage and more than 100 active users.
It served as a shared space for secure data exchange, templates, reporting, onboarding information, and a CRC-wide knowledge base.
The project also supported data management plans, data protection and governance coordination, and access to centralised data storage, archiving and HPC resources.
A particularly visible example was the integration of about 2,600 electronic health records with myocardial MRI data into a dedicated data warehouse with self-service query functionality and integrated data provenance tracking~\cite{johns_prov_tracking_2025}.
Another important line of work concerned the secure exchange of imaging data and the setup of interfaces and transfer workflows for large multimodal datasets across the CRC. The project was closely connected to local and national research data infrastructures, especially \href{https://www.nfdi4health.de/}{NFDI4Health} \cite{fluck_bausteine_2021} and the Medical Informatics Initiative \cite{prasser_difuture_2018}.
Locally, it strengthened connections between medical informatics, radiology, the IT department, and CRC subprojects by embedding service development directly into day-to-day research workflows. A key lesson was that a single unified RDM platform for all groups was not realistic because requirements, data types, and established practices differed too much between projects.
Also, corresponding processes were already established in the first funding phase, in which no INF project was included.
The project, therefore, shifted from a one-size-fits-all model to a hybrid, federated approach combining shared infrastructure with project-specific support.
Easier to adopt were pragmatic services with immediate benefit, especially the cooperation platform, shared documentation, and targeted support for concrete integration needs.
Overall, the project showed that sustainable INF work in a CRC requires both technical infrastructure and continuous hands-on support close to the researchers.
Its usefulness was reflected in the successful evaluation of the CRC for a third funding period, with relevant parts of INF being carried forward as part of a new subproject.

\subsection{Project INF --- Sustainable Research Data Management, \href{https://gepris.dfg.de/gepris/projekt/417590517?language=en}{CRC~1415 -  Chemistry of Synthetic Two-Dimensional Materials}, TUD Dresden University of Technology}
\label{subsec:crc1415}
The CRC~1415 "Chemistry of Synthetic Two-Dimensional Materials" established 2020 at TU Dresden operates at the intersection of chemistry, materials science, and physics
 aiming for controlled "bottom-up" synthesis and development of novel synthetic two-dimensional materials with high structural definition at the atomic or molecular level.
Since 2024, at the start of the second funding period, the INF project accompanied the central project to develop and implement sustainable RDM
infrastructure to support reproducibility and collaboration across 25 scientific projects and multiple partner institutions (Leibniz/Helmholtz) fostering harmonised data management strategies.
The primary focus has been on RDM—installing and maintaining scalable infrastructure
(\eg, local \href{https://nomad-lab.eu/nomad-lab/nomad-oasis.html}{NOMAD Oasis} of \href{https://www.fairmat-nfdi.eu/fairmat/}{FAIRmats} \href{https://nomad-lab.eu/nomad-lab/nomad.html}{NOMAD} platform),
integrating ELNs (\eg, \elabftw),
and providing regular training workshops for doctoral researchers and project leaders on FAIR data principles and infrastructure use.
RSE tasks, such as developing data pipelines, writing file parsers for proprietary formats as well as automatic data processing, complement these efforts.
The CRC\,1415 and the INF project are actively embedded in national and international networks,
including the NFDI, particularly \href{https://www.fairmat-nfdi.eu/}{FAIRmat} and \href{https://nfdi4chem.de/}{NFDI4Chem}, \href{https://dini.de/}{DINI}, \href{https://www.langzeitarchivierung.de}{nestor}, and the \href{https://www.rda-deutschland.de/}{RDA Germany},
which collectively facilitate knowledge exchange and the adoption of RDM best practices.
Locally, the INF project has strong institutional connections with TU Dresden’s CIDS ZIH (Center for Information Services and High Performance Computing)
and Service Center Research Data, DRESDEN-concept, and the Saxon State and University Library Dresden (SLUB)
combining technical, infrastructural, and domain-specific knowledge.
A significant challenge has been the consistent implementation of FAIR data principles and harmonised metadata standards across the CRC’s heterogeneous experimental and theoretical projects,
as well as the unified rollout of the ELN \elabftw ~and local data sharing platform \href{https://nomad-lab.eu/nomad-lab/nomad-oasis.html}{NOMAD Oasis}, due to diverse workflows and technical requirements.
In contrast, open-access paper publishing has been widely implemented and open-access data publishing via \href{https://zenodo.org/communities/crc1415/about}{Zenodo}, \href{https://opara.zih.tu-dresden.de/home}{OPARA}, and \href{https://rodare.hzdr.de/communities/crc1415}{RODARE} grows in adoption,
directly supporting the scientific outreach.
The experience in CRC~1415 underscores that technical infrastructure alone is insufficient; sustained training, community engagement, and the early embedding of RDM and RSE expertise within the CRC’s data life cycle are essential for the successful and sustainable adoption of open science practices and FAIR research activities.

\subsection{Project INF --- Data Management for Reproducible Data-Driven Computational Modeling Cross-Species and Cross-Labs, \href{https://gepris.dfg.de/gepris/projekt/458705875?language=en}{CRC~1451 - Key Mechanisms of Motor Control in Health and Disease}, University of Cologne, European Neuroscience Institute Göttingen, Goethe University Frankfurt, Hebrew University of Jerusalem, University of Münster, German Center for Neurodegenerative Diseases, Forschungszentrum Jülich, Max Planck Institute for Biology of Ageing, Max Planck Institute for Metabolism Research}
This INF project operates at the interface of computational neuroscience, data management, and scientific computing, supporting the overarching goal of CRC~1451 to understand motor control across biological scales and species.
The INF project provides expertise, tools, services, and training for reproducibly accessing, describing, and modelling data generated across all projects, including the central service projects Z02 and Z03.
A key task is the continuous assessment of workflows, resource requirements, and data analysis practices to identify differences that may affect data comparability and reusability.
The INF project is embedded in a broad network within the CRC~1451, working closely with experimental and computational projects across all domains and acting as a central hub for data handling and exchange.
\href{https://www.datalad.org/}{DataLad} is the main RDM solution adopted by the CRC to introduce structure and version control, ensure reproducibility, and support collaboration on heterogeneous data and workflows across projects with very different experimental setups and analysis methods.

\subsection{Project INF --- FAIR collection, curation and management of multidimensional research data, \href{https://gepris.dfg.de/gepris/projekt/506711657?language=en}{CRC 1625 – Atomic-scale understanding and design of multifunctional compositionally complex solid solution surfaces}, Ruhr University Bochum}
The CRC~1625 project started in 2024 and is dedicated to the investigation of compositionally complex solid solutions (CCSS) comprising five or more different elements mixed within a simple single-phase crystal structure.
Its objective is the design of surface atom arrangements (SAA), which have the potential to overcome the limitations of conventional elemental or binary electrocatalysts, thereby paving the way toward sustainable materials with unprecedented activity and stability.
As its RDMS platform, the INF project uses the \href{https://matinf.pro/}{MatInf} system, extending it with project-specific data types and actively contributing to its further development together with the INF project of TRR 247 (for example, MatInf has recently been migrated to the ASP.NET Core 10 + SQL Server 2025 technology stack).
The flexible methodology for the incremental evolution of RDM systems implemented in MatInf \cite{Dudarev2026} enables chemical entities to be treated as first-class data citizens while dynamically introducing new types of research data and deeply integrating them into the system.
This includes API-based integration of external services for validation, extraction, and visualization of data originating from newly introduced research document types.
A distinctive feature of CRC~1625 is that, alongside numerous projects dedicated to various aspects of CCSS/SAA research (including computational materials science), there is a group of projects directly dependent on the work of the INF project.
In this context, INF acts as a direct data provider for projects A05 (Materials Informatics: Data-guided experimentation and machine learning) and A06 (Computer Science: Establishing a knowledge graph for the design of CCSS surfaces).
All projects are provided with API-based access to the RDMS, including the possibility of executing arbitrary read queries through read-only database views.
This ensures flexible data selection capabilities for analytical workflows in project A05 and extensive opportunities for project A06 to build a knowledge graph on top of the data contained within the RDMS \cite{Mohamed2026}.
In addition to traditional workshops and hands-on sessions, the INF project, in collaboration with the Software Engineering Chair at Ruhr University Bochum, also offers a RDM course for students of the Computer Science faculty.
Within this course, MatInf serves as one of the central use cases, and students gain not only practical experience working with the system through its REST APIs, but are also deeply involved in evaluating the compliance of such systems with the FAIR principles and discussing possible directions for their future development.

\subsection{Project INF --- Scientific services and data management - \href{https://gepris.dfg.de/gepris/projekt/537324246?language=en}{CRC 1629 Negation in Language and Beyond} - Frankfurt am Main, Tübingen, Göttingen}
CRC 1629 \textit{Negation in Language and Beyond} (NegLaB) investigates negation as a grammatical and cognitive phenomenon from different empirical and theoretical perspectives. 
Its projects span a variety of disciplines, such as theoretical linguistics, corpus-based research as well as experimental work, resulting in a heterogeneous range of methodologies and workflows.
This diversity challenges and inspires the three principal investigators, Alexander Mehler (computer science), Manfred Sailer, and Sol Lago (linguistics), alongside fellow researchers from these disciplines, including Giuseppe Abrami, Nasimeh Bahmanian, Leon Hammerla, Andy Lücking, and Mercedes Martinez Bruera.

Our project fulfils a central mission by providing services such as research data management, consulting, and teaching, as well as software development.
Since the research questions explicitly require the integration of various cross-linguistic and cross-methodological models and concepts, it is crucial to ensure comparability and the ability to reuse data across different projects.
For this purpose, various platforms are provided as container-based solutions (in Docker and Kubernetes) to ensure sustainability and portability, as well as to extend existing implementations.
Our \href{https://about.gitlab.com/}{\textit{GitLab}} instance facilitates collaborative software development, while the web-based \href{https://nextcloud.com}{\textit{Nextcloud}} platform enables the storage of research results and provides centralized data backup.
Concurrently, a container-based server solution was developed for data acquisition, allowing experiments to be configured, shared, and executed online using open-source software such as PCIbex~\cite{Zehr:Schwarz:2018}.
Furthermore, localized large language models (LLMs) and visual language models (VLMs) can be accessed via an \href{https://openwebui.com/}{\textit{OpenWebUI}} instance utilising a dedicated Nvidia H200 GPU.
In addition, a \href{https://jupyter.org/hub}{\textit{Jupyter Hub}} (with Single Sign-On) is available for implementing web-based scripts and performing analyses using different models.
These advancements contribute to the instantiation of reusable software components within the distributed big data infrastructure \textit{DUUI}~\cite{Leonhardt:et:al:2023}, which enables container-based NLP methods, within cluster solutions~\cite{Abrami:et:al:2025:a}.
Through DUUI, heterogeneous implementations of methods, data formats~\cite{Hammerla:et:al:2025a}, and modalities~\cite{Bundan:Abrami:Mehler:2025} can be effectively processed, whereby a web- and API-based solution is also available through \textit{DUUIgateway}~\cite{Borkowski:et:al:2026}.

For versioning and repurposing of research results, a web-based metadata platform was developed that allows the flexible documentation of various types of information, including theoretical, experimental, and fieldwork-based materials.
Simultaneously, in collaboration with the library services at Goethe University Frankfurt, a CRC-wide data management protocol was developed and data management plans were established for each project using the Research Data Management Organiser (\href{https://rdmo.uni-frankfurt.de/}{RDMO})~\cite{Bahmanian:et:al:2025}.
This collaboration has also helped increase the visibility of INF’s research data management initiatives, as these have been presented and discussed at events organised by the library services. 

Our main takeaway is that an INF project is crucial in a humanities CRC: it helps ensure that sensitive and valuable data are handled appropriately, while at the same time giving researchers access to forms of training they would otherwise rarely receive. 
We have also learned how productive collaboration between computer scientists and linguists can be, as linguists provide fine-grained and theoretically grounded insights into language, while computer scientists contribute more efficient implementations and novel methodological approaches.

\subsection{Project INF --- Information Infrastructure, \href{https://www.sfb1638.de/}{CRC~1638 --- Cellular Membrane Remodelling -- How Changing Form Creates Function}, Heidelberg, Mannheim}
CRC~1638 investigates cellular membrane remodelling across five institutions, with projects generating data spanning imaging, structural biology, lipidomics, proteomics, and simulations.

Having a research background can be an asset here, helping to frame RDM in terms of workflows researchers recognise, though good RDM support takes many forms.
Investing in networks --- local data stewards, a university-wide ELN community, international practitioner groups --- substantially extends what a small project team can achieve.

These activities are particularly aimed at preparing early-career researchers, such as PhD students, for data management responsibilities. In addition, we offer comprehensive \href{https://purl.org/crc1646/INF/guidelines}{guidelines} and a \href{https://purl.org/crc1646/INF/FAQ}{FAQ section} on our \href{https://purl.org/crc1646/INF}{website}, enabling members to access relevant information as needed. In accordance with university policies, INF maintains close collaboration with the \href{https://www.uni-bielefeld.de/ub/digital/forschungsdaten/kompetenzzentrum/}{Competence Center of Research Data} and \href{https://www.uni-bielefeld.de/einrichtungen/bits/ueber-uns/index.xml}{Bielefeld IT Service (BITS)}. 
\textbf{III.} Finally, INF provides training in statistical methodologies through workshops and hands-on sessions. We also offer workshops and consultation on programming and annotation tools to support researchers in their technical work. In addition, our legal colleagues in INF offer guidance on the legal aspects of consent forms, highlighting key requirements and best practices through dedicated workshops and consultation sessions.

CIDS integrates expertise across fields such as High-Performance Computing, data analytics, and Artificial Intelligence and addresses complex challenges in research, education, and technology.
Through its innovative approach and strong interdisciplinary connections, CIDS, and particularly the ZIH-VDR, contribute significantly
to TU Dresden’s reputation in research excellence and digital transformation by addressing the challenges of the data-driven era of science.

\section{Lessons Learnt}
\label{sec:lessons}
As we have seen, the breadth of the INF projects across the scientific domains is very diverse.
Nevertheless, there are core questions that each INF project tries to find answers to.
In the in-person workshop, we led discussions in four separate groups.
We summarise each discussion in the following paragraphs.

\subsection{Outreach, Collaboration and Networking}

INF projects are often one of the cross-cutting components within a CRC,
and therefore they play an important part in the collaboration and networking within the project.
Since partners in INF projects necessarily need a higher-level overview of the work across the entire CRC, 
they can also be valuable partners or even facilitators of project outreach. 
This section discusses experiences in this important social and cross-disciplinary component of an INF project.

We acknowledge that some of the experiences likely only apply to certain categories of domains.
However, we also expect that often these are wide enough, \eg~“the natural sciences”, to be interesting to the reader.
Investigating the breadth of applicability would be valuable to do, however we do not have enough data to make such claims. 
Thus, most of the following has a more anecdotal character. Nevertheless, participants expressed that sharing these experiences 
was valuable for them, and likely also their projects. 

We start with the usual suspects and their perceived effectiveness, rooted in project management.
Most projects hold annual status and PhD candidate meetings.
Both are seen as somewhat useful, but their frequency is perceived as too low.
In contrast, quarterly meetings are experienced as much more effective: 
three months is not too often to interfere with “regular work”, 
but frequent enough to provide early feedback and incentivise timely implementation of agreed-upon targets.
Importantly, this frequency is not only seen as more effective by the INF teams,
but also by the researchers, increasing their engagement with the INF projects.
Relatedly, open development, as opposed to a closed one, eased outreach for multiple INF projects,
as researchers felt more connected to the work within the INF project. 

Another topic besides meeting frequency is the choice of contact channels.
Workshop participants agreed that there is no single preferred channel; rather
the best choice is effectively dictated by the channels the researchers within the CRC already use.
One piece of advice from the workshop is therefore: investigate the preferred communication and outreach channels of your community, and use those. 
While creating networks between non-INF projects was often experienced as easier than between INF and non-INF projects, serving and then re-using their communication channels was often helpful.

Generally, meeting formats with a lower (near-work) level seem to work far better than higher-level formats.
This includes offerings as part of PhD-schools, courses, as well as internal project pairings.
The closer those are to what researchers perceive as “the actual work”, the more successful they are.
One of the prerequisites for success, also more generally,
of these efforts seems to be an explicit backing of such low-level offerings and pairings by the project PIs.
One result is that new topics within digital research, \eg, tools or methods, can sometimes be relatively easy to introduce on a low-level, but may require substantial persuasion on the level of research-PIs.
One takeaway point here is that this PI-buy-in is not only helpful, but often inevitable.

Not surprisingly, general ideas that are close to the Open Science community, and thus to the RDM and RSE communities as well, like the FAIR principles,
can be hard to convey within some CRCs, especially when the served communities do not put a high value on those themselves.
There, an INF project often not only has the role as RDM/RSE-enabling unit, but also as an Open Science evangelist.

\subsection{How do we educate scientists and ourselves?}
Education and training for scientists and research data managers is a multifaceted challenge that goes beyond simply providing access to courses.
We acknowledge that an important task of INF projects is the provision of teaching materials, but the training of the INF project itself is equally important.
While many researchers are capable of identifying and enrolling in online training opportunities independently, a recurring difficulty lies in finding relevant and high-quality training that aligns with their specific needs and contexts.
This highlights the importance of not only curating suitable offerings but also guiding researchers toward them.
A key factor in effective training is the careful tailoring of content to the target audience.
Generic training formats often fall short, particularly when domain-specific tools and practices are involved.
Integrating such specialised tools into training programs remains a significant challenge,
yet it is essential for ensuring applicability in real-world research settings.
Without this alignment, there is a risk of participants losing interest and adopting suboptimal or ``wrong'' solutions early on. This leads to a kind of ``Good-Enough'' problem,
where initial, partial success obscures underlying issues and may even have long-term negative consequences.

To address these challenges, training efforts should be complemented by consulting services that engage directly with researchers’ specific use cases.
This allows for more targeted support and helps bridge the gap between theoretical knowledge and practical implementation.
In many cases, basic training is already provided by INF teams themselves, but there is clear value in expanding beyond these internal efforts.
Leveraging external training opportunities -- such as those offered externally to the CRC or broader initiatives -- can help diversify and strengthen the available training landscape.
An important dimension is the relationship between different communities, particularly between INF projects and domain researchers.
In some fields, formal certification processes are difficult to establish, partly due to mutual uncertainties and a lack of shared understanding.
This underscores the need for improved communication and information exchange between these communities.
Training is not only relevant for researchers but also for RSEs, Data Stewards, and other support roles, whose professional development is equally important for sustaining high-quality research environments.
In some cases, dedicated positions exist with explicit responsibility for training, extending beyond purely infrastructure-related topics.
However, these roles are often developed “on the job,” and a recurring issue is the lack of recognition through traditional academic outputs such as publications.
Overall, strengthening education and training in this space requires a combination of better curation,
audience-specific design, integration of domain tools, and closer alignment with real research practices.
Equally important are structural improvements, such as fostering cross-community exchange and recognising training-related work as a valuable professional contribution.

\subsection{Reproducibility, Quality and Validation of created Artefacts}
The workshop discussions established that while automated tools are rapidly advancing, human expertise remains central to artefact validation.
In fields dealing with sensitive clinical or animal data, researchers are increasingly adopting a "human-in-the-loop" approach.
Within this framework, machine learning models and locally-hosted or privacy preserving Large Language Models (LLMs) are utilised to suggest metadata or classify complex datasets, which are then verified through expert visualisation and manual checks.
This method ensures that data privacy is maintained while leveraging AI to handle the increasing volume of research outputs.
Technical validation becomes more complex as software grows in scale, requiring sophisticated containerisation and environment management.
Participants identified tools such as Pixi and the MaRDI packaging system as essential for managing dependencies and namespaces in large-scale projects.
For dynamic datasets that change over time, static identifiers alone are often insufficient to guarantee reproducibility.
Instead, modular Jupyter notebooks and data chunking strategies are used to document specific states and edge cases, ensuring results can be reconstructed even when the underlying data are not static.
Furthermore, implementing CI/CD pipelines and automated testing allows for the continuous monitoring of software quality, specifically checking for breaking changes or inconsistencies caused by random seeds and stochastic processes.
Finally, the long-term quality of artefacts depends on the rigorous use of ELNs like \href{https://chemotion.net/}{Chemotion}, \elabftw, and Sciformation to capture  experimental procedures and data provenance at the point of origin.
Integrating ELNs with established repositories like \href{https://dataverse.org/}{Dataverse}/\href{https://darus.uni-stuttgart.de/dataverse/darus}{DaRUS} enables structured, metadata-rich publication of experimental data, while version control platforms like GitHub support the parallel management of research software.
Despite these tools, the workshop highlighted a persistent gap in the quality of researcher-provided metadata, pointing to the need for better training and workflow integration to ensure artefacts remain verifiable and reusable over the long term.

\subsection{Workflows and Pipelines}
A common topic occurring in INF projects is helping researchers form pipelines of the software they use.
In this endeavour, especially workflow platforms are particularly beneficial.
Established examples from various domains are \href{https://galaxyproject.github.io/}{Galaxy}, \href{https://esciencelab.org.uk/products/taverna/}{Taverna}, and \href{https://workflowhub.eu/}{workflowhub}.
The use of standardised languages like \href{https://www.commonwl.org/}{CWL} would be beneficial, but so far we see a number of languages
like Mardiflow, \href{https://snakemake.readthedocs.io/en/stable/}{snakemake} and nextflow in use.\\
Another useful mechanism for automated workflows is pipelines,
as offered by platforms such as github and gitlab in their CI/CD environments.
While it would be great to use standardised languages, there are a couple of stumbling blocks that make adoption difficult:\\
Workflow languages require training.
Since you have to lay out your steps in a standardised language, you also have to learn it.
This process is similar to programming, and thus requires coding practice.
Codified pipelines will have version changes, and that can bring the well-known problem of long-term maintenance.
For some, an issue can be that workflows have to be permitted in the respective domain,
so it can be that the use of a workflow requires approvals, \eg~by an ethics board or similar.
While certainly appealing to INF projects, the participants noted that
workflow languages currently definitely have a low reach into the user base.\\
Other topics that hinder adoption are the availability of the inputs to the workflows.
Findability of the respective responsible users can be a problem, or
it can be difficult to get the permission to use input data.
% Re-Use and Maintenence are an issue.
Limited awareness of automation benefits often prevents people from getting started.
Possible solutions that the participants discussed are the use of an RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) supported Chat-Agent for scientists,
or easy/good documentation.

\section{Summary}
Comparing the initial problems of the INF projects in \autoref{sec:history}
with the actual tasks that INF projects perform in \autoref{sec:inf-in-germany}
and what we identified as ongoing challenges in the workshop in \autoref{sec:lessons}
we observe that technical challenges have evolved, while the human factor still is tricky ---  noting that no one reported on
the failure of an INF project \cite{Dierkes2020}.
We observe that the provision of long-term storage does not pose such a large issue anymore.
This has often either been solved within the CRC or through collaborations with institutions within the universities or within the framework of the NFDI.
This points to a greater shift in the landscape surrounding the INF projects.
Libraries and compute centres themselves offer to become part of the CRC proposals as evidenced by examples from \autoref{subsec:vdr}, the literature \cite{Hausen_Rosenberg_Trautwein-Bruns_Schwarz_2020},
and generalised into a template form in \cite{Kempf2026}.\\
Getting support as an INF project is an ongoing challenge that is helped by numerous networks.
The formation of the NFDI in 2020 brought a new push to the available networks for the INF projects, since
the consortia now form a domain specific network of sorts.
Also, numerous RDM specific networks emerged, like \href{https://saxfdm.de/}{SaxFDM}, that offer local, RDM specific networks.
INF projects leaning into software development have found networks in the \href{https://de-rse.org/}{RSE} movement.\\
Also in this manuscript, we have categorised INF projects where they lie on the RSE-RDM spectrum, but more broadly speaking,
these are all instances of \href{https://drtp-skills.ac.uk/}{dRTPs}, digital Research Technical Professionals.
INF projects will probably be even more diverse, if data scientists, AI specialists and other dRTPs can provide skills useful for the CRCs.
At the same time, INF projects increasingly fulfil an important mediating role between domain scientists, 
institutional infrastructure providers, and national initiatives such as the NFDI. Beyond providing technical solutions, 
they translate evolving requirements between communities with different practices, expectations, and technical backgrounds. 
This bridging function is becoming ever more critical as collaborative and data intensive research continues to grow in complexity.
So with respect to the communities and the professionalisation the INF projects have matured, but what has stayed the same?
INF projects in CRCs are subject to the project based funding of the DFG, therefore the CRC itself cannot provide a stable position.
This also brings up again the unresolved problem of how to provide continuity across personnel rotations in INF projects.
It is encouraging to see that libraries and compute centres are stepping up here, since these are institutions that
can provide long-term career perspectives for experts employed in INF projects and can provide a fallback in the case of rotations.
Hopefully, in the future more improvements can be made \eg, by combining the political voice of the data stewards and the RSE movement.\\
Comparing with \cite{mueckschel2006} a couple of other challenges still persist, such as INF projects struggling
to adapt their platforms to the scientists’ evolving requirements, or how to design platforms that are open and extensible
while still being easy enough to be accepted by users.
We have detailed some of these struggles and some of this generation’s solutions in our lessons learnt in \autoref{sec:lessons}.
Adapting to rapidly changing scientific questions will always be a challenge to the people filling out the role of the INF project in a CRC.
But together with the strong networks they are embedded in\footnote{
We founded a network for INF projects: \url{https://www.listserv.dfn.de/sympa/info/inf-projects-in-crcs}.
}, this is a challenge that INF projects everywhere happily take on.
